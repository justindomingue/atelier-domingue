# The Back — Construction Guide

---

## 1. Sewing Machine Tips

### Needles
- Use the largest needle your machine accepts. Home machines typically max out at size 110/80 heavy duty; industrial machines use 130/21.
- Both needle types have a groove along the back to help thread enter fabric without breaking.

### Thread
- Use 100% cotton thread: #30 (thinner) for the bobbin to reduce friction, #20 (slightly thicker) for the upper thread.
- If using polyester jeans thread (e.g. Gutermann), the same thread works for both upper and bobbin.
- Wax the thread end with beeswax if you have trouble threading the needle.

### Bobbin Tension
- You will likely need to adjust bobbin tension for denim and thicker threads.
- The bobbin casing should drop slightly when you let it fall from your hand while holding the thread.
- Turn the tension screw no more than **1/4 turn** at a time (most likely counter-clockwise to loosen). Be careful — the screw and spring can shoot out if loosened too far.
- Adjust upper tension to match, then test on scrap fabric.

### Testing Your Machine (Modern Construction)
- The modern flat-felled method requires sewing through **up to 16 layers** at the yoke and crotch junctions. (1873 method: ~9 layers.)
- Run the overlap/fold/topstitch test described in the course using scrap denim before starting on your jeans.
- Use a **seam jumper** at thick junctions to maintain stitch quality.
- Use a heavy **goose iron** (16 lb) after steaming to flatten thick junctions, followed by hammering over a block of wood.

## 2. Finishing the Raw Edges

*Applies to 1873 construction or if your machine can't handle flat-felled seams.*

- Trim the 3/8" extra at the back leg yoke seam (only needed for modern flat-felled construction).
- Overcast the following raw edges by hand (or use a serger):
  - Front leg inseam
  - Back leg inseam
  - Seat seam (from lower hip line to yoke)
  - Yoke seam
  - Yoke seat seam (longer side)
- Work "downhill" (from the higher part of each seam downward) to prevent thread from catching and unraveling the raw edge.
- Keep about **1/8" depth and distance** between overcasting stitches.
- Use the finger behind the cloth to guide the thread for neater stitches.
- Expect about **40–60 minutes per leg**.
- Press everything well afterward — denim tends to curl or stretch during overcasting.

---

## 3. The Back Yoke — 1873 Method

*For 1873 reproduction jeans, or if your machine can't handle the thicker modern seam junctions.*

- Lay out back legs and yokes, ensuring correct left/right pairing. The **long side of the yoke is the seat seam**.
- Place yoke on back leg, **right sides together**. Pin the far end first, aligning the seam at the edge.
- Baste the near end with three stitches, then baste the yoke seam using a **diagonal basting stitch** about 1/2" from the edge.
- As you baste, **distribute fullness** from the back leg evenly along the yoke length.
- Machine sew using a **3/8" seam allowance**, keeping the leg (with fullness) on the bottom so feed dogs help distribute it.
- Remove basting stitches.
- Press all yoke seams **down towards the leg** (away from the yoke). Keep the iron on the seam itself to avoid shrinking out the fullness.
- Top stitch with a single row, just under **1/8" from the edge**. Keep this spacing consistent throughout the project.
- Repeat for the other leg.

---

## 4. The Back Yoke — Modern Method (Flat-Felled)

*Requires a machine that can handle up to 16 layers of denim.*

- Mark a **3/8" seam allowance** along the bottom of the yoke.
- Lay the back leg on top of the yoke, **right sides up**. Overlap the back leg so it just touches the marked seam line.
- Sew down the middle of the overlap, about **3/16" from the edge**, aligning the denim as you go.

### Pressing
- Wrong side up: fold the yoke along the edge of the hidden yoke seam. Press with fingers, then iron with steam (keep iron on the seam only).
- Fold the yoke back to final position, press again with fingers and iron.
- Flip and press from the right side.

### Top Stitching
- First row: about **1/16" from the folded edge** through all layers.
- Second row: about **5/16" (just under 3/8")** from the first row. Make sure you're catching all layers underneath.

---

## 5. The Back Pockets

### Pocket Lining
- Mark **7/8"** along the top of the pocket lining, and **1/2" seam allowances** on sides and bottom.
- Trim the seam allowances from the lining.

### Preparing the Pocket
- On the right side of the pocket, mark **7/8"** along the top and **3/8"** along sides and bottom.
- Press the top edge over along the seam line (wrong side). Tuck the raw edge under to form a finished **3/8" seam**, and press.
- Add arcuate stitching if desired before attaching the lining.
- Center the lining on the wrong side, tucked under the top seam. Stitch arcuate from edge to edge, beginning and ending in the seam allowances.

### Top Stitching the Pocket Top
- **1873 jeans:** Single row, **3/8" from the edge**.
- **Modern jeans:** Two rows — first about **1/8" from edge**, second about **1/4"–3/8" from the first row** (match yoke spacing).

### Finishing
- Press sides and bottom edges toward the wrong side along your marked lines.
- The fabric point at top corners can be trimmed slightly, folded under, or tucked after top stitching.

---

## 6. Attaching the Back Pockets

*One pocket for 1873, two for modern.*

### Positioning
- Along the side seam, mark **2 1/2" from the selvedge edge**, just below the yoke.
- Measure down **2" from the yoke seam**.
- Align the right side of the pocket at that intersection; rotate the left side so it's about **1 1/2" below the yoke seam**.
- Trace the outline at the upper corners for alignment reference.

### Sewing
- Starting from the selvedge side, top stitch about **1/8" from the edge** along sides and bottom. Start with a **back tack** for strength.
- Turn stitching precisely at corners.
- Before stitching the remaining side, **slide the top corner toward the selvedge by ~1/8"** — this creates extra room for the pocket to curve smoothly around the seat.
- End with another back tack. **Leave threads long.**

### Second Row
- Mark **5/8" from each upper corner** at the top, tapering to about **1/4" from the stitching** about 2" below the pocket opening.
- Top stitch following the guideline: **1/4" spacing** (1873) or match yoke spacing (modern).
- **Leave threads long**, pull ends through to wrong side with a seam ripper, tie knots, and trim to 1/2".

## 7. 1873 Yoke Pleat

*For the straight-cut 1873 yoke only.*

- Align the yoke pattern on the yoke piece and mark two small marks at the top of the dart.
- Extend marks straight down about 1/2" for clarity.
- Pinch and crease the fabric at the mark closest to the selvedge/side seam.
- Fold so the creased edge touches the second mark.
- Sew across the top of the pleat, **1/4" from the waist seam** (stitches will be hidden by the waistband's 3/8" seam allowance).
- Repeat for the other side.

---

## 8. The Seat Seam — 1873 Method

- Lay back legs **right sides together**, aligning the seat seam. Re-mark the hip line at the seat seam if needed.
- Pin and sew a **5/8" seam** along the seat seam.
- **Stretch the crotch curve** gently in the seam allowance only (straightening it) using the iron. This helps the seam roll without tension.
- Press the seat seam **towards the left side**. Use a tailor's ham under curved areas.
- Flip to right side and press crisp.
- Top stitch along the entire seat seam about **1/16"–1/8" from the creased edge** (match yoke spacing).
- Starting from the crotch, add a **second row of top stitching** about 3/8" from the first row. Taper back into the first row of stitching at the hip point and back tack.

---

## 9. The Seat Seam — Modern Method (Flat-Felled)

- Trim the upper seat seam near the yoke flush if anything protrudes.
- On the right back leg, mark a **3/8" seam allowance** along the seat seam.
- Starting at the waist, align the left leg over the right, butting the raw edge just outside the marked line.
- Sew down the center of the overlap, aligning the yoke area as closely as possible.
- Continue through the curved area, manipulating fabric carefully to stay aligned.

### Pressing
- Right sides together: align the hidden raw edge with the folded seat seam edge. Crease with fingers, then iron with steam.
- Ensure a good **3/8" overlap** in the curved area — fabric tends to unroll there.
- Use a **heavy goose iron** on the yoke junction.
- Flip wrong sides together and press again. Don't let the iron extend too far from the edge.
- Open legs right side up, manipulate seam into final position, and **hammer the yoke junction** over a block of wood.

### Top Stitching
- Starting at the crotch, top stitch about **1/8" from the creased edge** (match yoke distances). Use a **seam jumper** at the yoke junction.
- Second row: about **1/4"–3/8" from the first**, catching the seam below. Use the seam jumper again at the yoke.
- If stitches run off the seam anywhere, undo and redo — it's worth it.
- Note: yoke seams won't align perfectly at the junction — this is normal and a sign of good construction.

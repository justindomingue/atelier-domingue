# Drafting the Fly

Source: https://masterclasses.historical-tailoring.com/lesson/drafting-the-fly-and-waistband/

## 1873 Fly (Two-Piece / Historical)

This is as close as possible to the original 1873 fly. It is also useful for
those who do not want to use the one-piece fly.

### Steps

1. **Draw the construction line:** parallel to the front fly line, **1 3/4"**
   from the **seam line** (measured from the seam line itself, not from seam
   allowances).

2. **Draw the curve:** starting at the bottom of the fly extension, smoothing
   into the construction line.

3. **Copy the outline** to a fresh sheet.

4. **Add inlay:** approximately **1"** at the top edge, to be trimmed later.

### Cutting notes

- Cut **on the fold** (or flip the pattern over on the fold line and draw the
  other half).
- Optionally place one edge on the **selvedge** for a cleaner finish. When the
  full fly is opened up, the selvedge goes on the **left side**.

## One-Piece Fly (Modern / Post-1877)

The one-piece fly is very easy to draft and is the best and strongest choice
for any type of jean after approximately 1877.

### Steps

1. **Measure the fly extension** on the front pattern piece (the rise curve
   from 1\* to 7\*).

2. **Calculate the fly length:** double the fly extension measurement, then add
   **2"** for ease.

3. **Calculate the fly width:** recommended finished width **1 3/4"**.
   Total pattern piece width = **4 1/2"**, broken down as:

   | Section | Width |
   |---------|-------|
   | Seam allowance | 1/2" |
   | Front half | 1 3/4" |
   | **FOLD** | |
   | Back half | 1 3/4" |
   | Seam allowance | 1/2" |

### Practical tip

In practice, the rectangle can be drafted directly on the cloth without
separate construction lines.

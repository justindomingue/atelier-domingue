# Fitting & Cutting Guide — Selvedge Denim Jeans

---

## 1. Cutting the Jeans (from Denim)

### Pieces to Cut

- Front Legs
- Back Legs
- Yoke (straight or curved depending on pattern)
- Back Pockets (only one for 1873 jeans)
- Front Pocket Facings
- Watch Pocket
- Pocketing Pieces / Pocket Bags
- Back Pocket Linings (cut same as back pockets)

### Layout Tips

- Cut legs on the selvedge. Waistband and fly can go on selvedge too if fabric allows.
- Because of selvedge denim's nature, trace one pattern piece, flip it, and trace the second side on the reverse — don't cut both at once.
- Keep pieces facing the same direction (some weaves have a directional pattern).
- Cut smaller pieces (pockets, facings) from areas between the legs.
- Pocket bags are cut on the fold as indicated in the pattern.

### Marking & Cutting Tips

- Weigh down pattern pieces and use chalk to mark edges for accuracy.
- Lay long side seams directly on the selvedge edge to prevent fraying.
- Make small chalk marks at the hem, knee, crotch, and hip lines to aid construction.
- Don't clip notches — it ruins the selvedge. Chalk marks are easy to re-add if they fade.
- Save leftover selvedge strips for belt loops (modern pairs).
- Roll finished pieces into a bundle to avoid creases if not sewing right away.

## 2. Fitting Alterations

After sewing and trying on the muslin toile, use the following adjustments as needed. For all alterations: redraw curves smoothly, maintaining right angles where they exist in the original draft.

### Waist Width

- Determine the total width to add or subtract, then **divide by four**.
- Apply one quarter to the front fly area, and one quarter to the back seat seam.
- On the front: adjust the fly area, tapering back into the hip line. Adjusting the curved side seam should be a last resort.
- On the back: add or subtract the amount and redraw the curves.
- Curve the lines more or less as needed for a smooth transition below the hip line.

### Waist Height

- If the waist sits too high or too low, move the two waist points up or down by the needed amount.
- Taper the curves back into the original draft.

### Hip Width

- Tightness when walking or sitting indicates the hips are too narrow.
- Add the necessary amount to the **side seams**.
- Redraw the front fly area and seat seam at the waist to remove the excess just added there.
- You may also need to adjust crotch width to account for added hip width — do a fitting first before deciding.

### Crotch Width

- The most common alteration. Symptoms: tight seat ("wedgie" effect) and tightness in front.
- **Back:** Move the back crotch point outward as needed (up to 1 1/2" in extreme cases). Redraw curves, maintaining the right angle.
- **Front:** Be more conservative — adjust 1/4" at a time. Redraw curves as necessary.

### Crotch Height

- Needed when the inseam measurement is slightly off (typically 1/4" to 1/2" adjustment).
- Lower or raise the crotch point by an **equal amount on both front and back legs**.
- Redraw the curves, maintaining the right angle.
